# Cones, temperature, and porcelain

## Cones measure heatwork, not temperature

Pyrometric cones bend when a target amount of **heatwork** (time × temperature) is reached —
not at a single temperature. Fire faster and a cone bends at a higher temperature; fire
slower (or hold/soak) and it bends lower. So "cone 6" is a *maturity*, and the °C below are
**approximate** (large cones, ~60 °C/hr). Use `glaze-calc`'s `convert.py cone <n>` for the
lookup. Concept: Orton cone charts; Glazy [temperature](https://help.glazy.org/concepts/temperature).

| Cone | ≈ °C | ≈ °F | Typical use |
|------|------|------|-------------|
| 06   | ~999  | ~1830 | Bisque, low-fire glaze |
| 04   | ~1060 | ~1940 | Low-fire glaze, commercial |
| 5    | ~1196 | ~2185 | Mid-range |
| **6**| **~1222** | **~2232** | **Mid-range — this studio's target** |
| 10   | ~1305 | ~2381 | High-fire stoneware/porcelain (often reduction) |

(°C vary ~10–20° with ramp rate; these are the ~60 °C/hr large-cone values used by
`convert.py`.)

## Cone 6 for porcelain in a home electric kiln

- **This studio fires cone 6 porcelain in an electric kiln (oxidation).** Cone 6 is the
  practical mid-range sweet spot for home studios: porcelain bodies sold for cone 6 vitrify
  to a hard, translucent-ish, low-absorption result, and cone 6 is far easier on kiln
  elements and shelves than cone 10.
- **Confirm the body is rated for the cone.** A cone-10 porcelain under-fired at cone 6 stays
  porous and weak; a cone-6 porcelain over-fired toward cone 10 can warp/bloat. Match clay,
  glaze, and firing to the same cone.
- **Bisque** is usually cone 06–04 (porous enough to soak glaze, fired hot enough to burn off
  organics) before the cone-6 glaze firing.
- **Cone 10 / reduction** is the classic high-fire porcelain world (gas kilns) — relevant as
  background and for understanding reduction colors, but **not** this electric-kiln workflow,
  except via **saggar local reduction** at cone 6 (see the `firing-and-atmosphere` skill).

## Firing-schedule levers at cone 6

Beyond peak cone, the schedule shapes results: a **hold/soak** near peak adds heatwork and
heals pinholes/bubbles; a controlled **slow cool** through ~1100→900 °C grows crystals and
develops mattes, rutile breaks, and some colors. For glaze fit, cooling rate also affects
body silica inversions. Treat schedule as part of the recipe.
