# Colorants in oxidation vs reduction

How much a colorant changes between an **oxidizing** atmosphere (normal electric kiln) and a
**reducing** atmosphere (gas reduction, or local reduction inside a **saggar**) depends on
whether the metal has accessible oxidation states that the atmosphere can flip. Below,
colorants are ranked from **most** to **least** atmosphere-sensitive, per Tony Hansen /
Digital Fire. Cite the linked page for any specific claim.

## Ranking (most → least atmosphere-sensitive)

### 1. Copper — the biggest shift (green ⇄ blood-red)
- **Oxidation:** CuO is stable and gives clear **greens** (turquoise with high alkali, green
  with calcia/zinc). Source: [digitalfire.com/oxide/cuo](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/cuo).
- **Reduction:** CuO converts to **Cu2O (cuprous oxide)** → **copper reds** ("blood-red",
  "ox-blood"/sang de boeuf, fire-red). Purples occur where oxidized-green and reduced-red
  copper mix. This is the single most dramatic colorant response to atmosphere.
- Tiny amounts (often <1%) make copper reds; they are notoriously hard to control and
  reoxidize easily on cooling. See [oxide/cuo](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/cuo),
  [glossary/reduction+firing](https://digitalfire.com/glossary/reduction+firing).

### 2. Iron — huge shift in BOTH color and melt behavior
- **Oxidation:** Fe2O3 (ferric) behaves as a **refractory antiflux**; color runs amber/honey
  (≤~4%), tan (~6%), to brown and saturated red-brown/black at higher additions.
- **Reduction:** Fe2O3 → **FeO (ferrous)**, which is a **powerful flux** — the melt becomes
  more fluid *and* the color turns cool: **celadons** (0.5–2% iron, green-blue) and
  **tenmoku/iron saturate** (8–10%) blacks breaking rust where thin. A body that fires light
  tan in oxidation can fire deep brown in reduction.
- So iron changes both *what color* and *how much it melts*. Source:
  [digitalfire.com/oxide/fe2o3](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/fe2o3).

### 3. Titanium / rutile / ilmenite — moderate; titanium **does** react
- **Yes, titania reacts to atmosphere.** Hansen: TiO2 "can be reduced to produce colors in
  keeping with the elements present. If highly reduced it can yield a red; with iron the
  color could be yellow, brown or green." It is also slightly phototropic/thermotropic.
  Source: [digitalfire.com/oxide/tio2](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/tio2).
- But titania's *main* jobs are physical, not its own color: it drives **crystallization,
  opacity, and variegation/mottling** (effect grows up to ~10%), and it **modifies the colors
  of other metals** (Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu). In a saggar with iron present, expect titania
  to push toward warm mottled yellows/browns and encourage visual texture.
- **Rutile** and **ilmenite** are iron-bearing titanium minerals, so they carry both
  titanium's variegation and iron's atmosphere sensitivity (see TiO2 vs rutile below). The
  Digital Fire ilmenite page was not confirmed in research — verify before citing it
  specifically; the iron+titania reasoning is sound.

### 4. Manganese — mild shift
- **Oxidation:** browns and (alumina-free, alkaline) purples/violets; blacks at high % with
  other colorants. **Reduction:** Hansen notes "manganous oxide is unaffected by reduction"
  in the sense that it stays MnO; practically, reduction tends toward muted tan-browns
  ("pleasing tan-brown reduction glazes with ~5% manganese dioxide"). Net: present but mild.
  Source: [digitalfire.com/oxide/mno](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/mno). **Safety:** manganese
  fumes are a respiratory hazard — good ventilation, especially relevant in saggar work where
  combustibles and metal salts off-gas.

### 5. Cobalt, chrome, nickel — essentially atmosphere-stable
- **Cobalt (CoO):** "very color dependable under both oxidizing and reducing conditions."
  The blue's *shade* depends on the **host glaze chemistry** (magnesia → pink/lilac, etc.),
  not the atmosphere. [oxide/coo](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/coo).
- **Chrome (Cr2O3):** a "fast" colorant that "produces its characteristic green in slow or
  fast and oxidizing or reducing firing." Very refractory and stable.
  [oxide/cr2o3](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/cr2o3).
- **Nickel (NiO):** highly variable, but the variation is driven by **host chemistry**
  (lithium→yellow, zinc→blue, calcium→tan, barium→brown), not atmosphere.
  [oxide/nio](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/nio).

## Titanium dioxide vs rutile

| | **Titanium dioxide (TiO2)** | **Rutile (ceramic)** |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | Refined, ~pure titania | Impure **iron-bearing titania ore** (plus trace Nb, V, etc.) |
| Color of its own | Essentially white/none; relies on crystallization | Tan/amber from its **iron** content |
| Effect | Predictable opacity, crystallization, fine mottling | Stronger, warmer **variegation/streaking/"rutile break"**, plus iron color |
| Reliability | Consistent batch to batch | Hansen: "the least reliable option for coloring glazes" — **varies by source/lot** |
| Atmosphere | Reacts mainly via reduction of Ti | Reacts via **both** its Ti and its Fe — more atmosphere-sensitive than pure TiO2 |

Practical takeaway: use **TiO2** when you want controllable opacity/crystallization and a
clean base for other colorants; use **rutile** when you specifically want that warm,
broken, variegated "rutile blue/green" surface — and accept lot-to-lot variation. A classic
**rutile blue** is built on a fluid base with a little cobalt and/or iron plus rutile or
TiO2; titania-driven micro-crystallization scatters light to read blue. Sources:
[material/rutile](https://digitalfire.com/material/rutile),
[oxide/tio2](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/tio2).

## Saggar note (this user's setup)

Firing an electric kiln (oxidation) but using **saggars packed with combustibles** creates a
*local* reducing/“flashing” atmosphere around the pot. That means the atmosphere-sensitive
colorants above — **copper (reds), iron (celadon/FeO flux, dark flashing), titania/rutile
(warm mottling)** — become reachable even without a gas kiln, while cobalt/chrome/nickel will
look much as they do in oxidation. Localized, uneven reduction is the *point* of saggar work,
so expect variegation and one-of-a-kind results rather than uniform color. See the
`firing-and-atmosphere` skill for how the saggar atmosphere actually forms.
