# Saggar firing: atmosphere, trapped gases, and water vapor

A **saggar** is a closeable refractory container you load ware into, traditionally to protect
it from flame/ash, and in studio practice to **trap a local atmosphere** (combustibles +
colorant salts) so the pot inside reduces/flashes while the kiln around it stays oxidizing.
This is how this studio gets reduction effects at **cone 6 in an electric kiln**.

## What's in the saggar atmosphere

As the saggar heats, in rough order:
1. **Residual moisture** (in the combustibles, any soluble salts, the saggar/ware itself)
   boils off as **steam (H2O vapor)** well below 200 °C.
2. **Organics combust/pyrolyze**: they first burn in the trapped O2 (→ CO2 + H2O), and once
   the oxygen is used up they **smolder/char**, producing **CO** and leaving **carbon**.
3. **Colorant salts/carbonates** decompose, releasing their own gases and depositing metal
   onto the surface (copper, iron → flashing).

So the late-firing saggar interior is a hot mix of **CO, CO2, H2O vapor, H2, and carbon** —
strongly reducing. *(This sequence is standard combustion chemistry + studio practice;
treat as reasoned, not a single Digital Fire citation.)*

## The water-vapor question at cone 6 (~1225 °C)

**Q: Does trapped water vapor dissociate at cone 6? Does it stay trapped? Does reduction
change this?**

1. **Thermal dissociation of H2O is negligible at cone 6.** Splitting water into H2 + O2
   purely by heat (`2 H2O → 2 H2 + O2`) needs far higher temperatures — meaningful thermal
   dissociation is a >2000 °C phenomenon. At ~1225 °C the fraction that splits on its own is
   vanishingly small. So **no, the water molecule does not just fall apart at cone 6.**
   *(Reasoned from thermodynamics — established physical chemistry, not a Digital Fire page.)*

2. **But water DOES react chemically in a reducing, carbon-rich saggar.** With hot carbon
   present, the **water-gas reaction** runs:
   ```
   C + H2O → CO + H2          (endothermic; proceeds at red heat with carbon)
   CO + H2O ⇌ CO2 + H2        (water-gas shift)
   ```
   This consumes water vapor and **produces more CO and H2 — both reducing gases.** So in a
   saggar packed with combustibles, residual moisture doesn't just sit there inertly; to the
   extent it meets glowing carbon it gets converted into *more* reducing atmosphere. Reduction
   doesn't make water "dissociate" thermally, but the reducing/carbon environment is exactly
   what lets water participate via these reactions. *(Reasoned chemistry; the water-gas and
   shift reactions are standard, well outside Digital Fire's scope.)*

3. **Does it stay trapped until you open the saggar?** Not entirely. A saggar is **not
   gas-tight** — lids and seams leak, and the ware/saggar are porous. Gas volume expands
   ~5× from room temp to 1225 °C, so internal pressure pushes the lighter gases (H2O, H2, CO)
   out through gaps over the firing. What remains and matters for your pot is the **reducing
   condition and the metal vapors deposited on the surface**, not a literal trapped puddle of
   steam. By the time you open a cooled saggar, the vapor is long gone; you're looking at the
   flashing/reduction it helped create. *(Reasoned: gas laws + the fact that saggars are
   deliberately not hermetic.)*

**Bottom line:** at cone 6, trapped water stays molecular (it won't thermally dissociate),
mostly vents as steam, and where it contacts hot carbon it feeds the water-gas reactions that
*strengthen* reduction. The reducing atmosphere doesn't "trap" it so much as **consume and
vent** it.

## Practical loading & control

- You tune saggar results by **what and how much** you pack (sawdust, paper, charcoal; copper
  carbonate, iron, salt, seaweed for flashing), how tightly the pot is wrapped, and how well
  the lid seals — not by a kiln damper.
- Bisque the saggar contents' moisture down first; excessive water/organics can cause uneven
  results and more smoke.
- Don't seal a glaze over reduction too early — trapped gas under a melted glaze causes
  **bloating/pinholing**. Saggar reduction pairs most predictably with the surface/flashing
  and with glazes/slips chosen to take it.

## Safety (do not skip)

- **Carbon monoxide is toxic and odorless.** Combustibles in a kiln produce CO; fire with
  **strong ventilation** (vent system / well-ventilated space), never in a closed room.
- **Fumes:** copper, manganese, chlorides/salts and smoke are hazardous to breathe — use
  appropriate ventilation and avoid manganese-heavy saggar mixes without it.
- **Kiln wear:** in-chamber combustion stresses elements and brick (see
  `reduction-mechanism.md`). Confirm your kiln manufacturer's guidance before routine saggar
  firing in an electric kiln.
