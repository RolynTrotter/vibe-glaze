# What reduction is, and how it's created

## The mechanism

A kiln atmosphere is **reducing** when it is **starved of oxygen** and contains **carbon
monoxide (CO)**. CO is "hungry" for an oxygen atom; to become the more stable CO2 it will
strip oxygen from wherever it can — including metal oxides in the glaze and clay body:

```
CuO  + CO  →  Cu2O (+ CO2)      copper: green  → blood-red
Fe2O3 + CO →  2 FeO (+ CO2)     iron:   amber/antiflux → FeO, a powerful flux (celadon/tenmoku)
```

So reduction changes results in two ways: it changes the **oxidation state (and color)** of
colorants, and — because the reduced forms (e.g. FeO) often behave differently — it changes
how the glaze **melts**. **Oxidation** is the opposite: an oxygen-rich atmosphere keeps metals
in their higher oxide state (CuO, Fe2O3). A normal **electric kiln fires in oxidation** —
elements, not flame, so there's plenty of air and no CO. Sources:
[glossary/reduction+firing](https://digitalfire.com/glossary/reduction+firing),
[glossary/oxidation+firing](https://digitalfire.com/glossary/oxidation+firing),
[oxide/cuo](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/cuo), [oxide/fe2o3](https://digitalfire.com/oxide/fe2o3).

## How reduction is normally produced (gas kilns)

In a fuel kiln you create reduction by **supplying more fuel than the available air can fully
burn** (rich combustion) — typically by cutting the air/damper. Incomplete combustion leaves
CO in the chamber. Potters use "body reduction" (heavier, ~900–1000 °C, affects the clay and
iron) and lighter "glaze reduction" through the rest of the climb. *(Citable: Digital Fire
reduction-firing glossary. The specific temperatures/strategy are conventional practice.)*

## Creating reduction in an ELECTRIC kiln — via combustibles / saggars

An electric kiln has no flame, so you must **introduce a combustible** that consumes oxygen
and releases CO/CO2 locally:

- **Local reduction:** seal organic material (sawdust, paper, dryer lint, banana skins,
  cones of charcoal) and often metal-bearing colorants (copper carbonate, salts) **inside a
  saggar** — a closeable refractory box — packed around the pot. As it heats, the
  combustibles burn the trapped O2, then smolder/pyrolyze, generating CO and carbon that
  reduce and "flash" the ware *inside the saggar* while the rest of the kiln stays oxidizing.
- This is how this studio gets reduction effects at **cone 6** without a gas kiln. It is
  inherently **local and uneven** — that variegated, one-off "flashing" is the aesthetic.

> Reasoned, not a Digital Fire citation: the saggar interior reduction follows from basic
> combustion chemistry (finite trapped O2 → CO once oxygen is depleted) and is well documented
> in studio practice; treat specifics (how much combustible, how reducing) as things to dial
> in by testing.

### Caveat: elements and electric kilns

Reduction and combustion by-products are **hard on kiln elements and brick** (oxide scale on
elements is protective; reduction/carbon can degrade it). Containing the combustion **inside a
saggar** is partly what protects the rest of the kiln — but expect some element wear and plan
ventilation. This is practical guidance, not a manufacturer guarantee; check your kiln maker's
stance on in-chamber combustibles.

## Why timing matters

Atmosphere only "counts" while the relevant reaction can happen. Color/melt-changing reduction
needs to occur while the ware is hot enough for the oxides to react and before the glaze seals
over (after which trapped reduction can cause bloating/pinholes). In saggar work you don't
control atmosphere with a damper; you control it by **what and how much** you pack in the
saggar and how well it's sealed. See `saggar-firing.md`.
